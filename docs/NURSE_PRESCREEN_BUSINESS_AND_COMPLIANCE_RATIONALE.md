# Why cannabis/nicotine clinics use nurse consultation before doctor review

**Status:** research / product planning only. Not legal advice. Not clinical protocol until reviewed by the responsible Australian clinician(s) and legal/compliance advisor.

**Scope:** Explains the practical, clinical, compliance and commercial rationale for a nurse prescreen layer in PouchCare-style regulated telehealth workflows.

---

## 1. Short answer

A nurse prescreen is usually **not legally required** in ordinary telehealth. The legally important requirement is that the doctor or authorised prescriber completes a proper real-time consultation, confirms identity, assesses suitability, and makes the prescribing decision.

Clinics use nurses before doctors because it improves the operating model:

1. lower cost per patient interaction
2. better doctor utilisation
3. better triage and fewer wasted doctor consults
4. cleaner clinical notes before doctor review
5. better patient education/support
6. safer escalation of obvious red flags
7. stronger evidence that the clinic is not just “questionnaire-to-script”
8. better patient conversion because the first human touchpoint is supportive and often free/low-cost

For PouchCare, nurse prescreen should be viewed as an **optional operational quality layer**, not a mandatory compliance layer.

## 2. Evidence from comparable clinics

### Alternaleaf

Alternaleaf publicly describes a model where patients start with a **complimentary 20-minute nurse consultation** before paying for a doctor consultation. Their pricing page says the nurse call is used to understand health needs, provide a safe/supportive space, and let patients ask questions. Their doctor consultation is listed as a separate paid 10-minute call to decide whether treatment is right.

Source:
- https://www.alternaleaf.com.au/pricing
- https://www.alternaleaf.com.au/process

Relevant extracted points:
- “complimentary assessment from one of our friendly nurses first”
- “Initial nurse consultation — Complimentary — 20-minute call to understand your health needs”
- “Doctor consultations — $29 — 10-minute call to see if treatment is right for you”
- Their prescreen questions include chronic condition duration, prior attempted treatment, whether treatment failed/caused side effects, pregnancy/breastfeeding/trying to conceive, and psychosis/schizophrenia/bipolar history.
- They state being eligible for a nurse consultation does not mean the patient will be prescribed medication.

Interpretation:
- This strongly suggests nurse consultation is used as a low-cost first clinical/support layer before the paid doctor decision point.
- It also helps filter out patients who should not proceed.

### Polln

Polln positions itself differently. Its pricing page says consultations are paid because they are delivered by AHPRA-registered doctors and designed to provide a thorough clinical assessment. Search result snippets also indicate Polln distinguishes itself from clinics where nurse consultations are the only option, while still offering ongoing nurse support.

Source:
- https://www.polln.com/pricing

Relevant extracted points:
- “Polln consultations are a paid medical service because they are delivered by AHPRA-registered doctors and designed to provide a thorough clinical assessment”
- “Each consultation includes a dedicated 30-minute session to review your medical history...”
- “ongoing, free nurse support” appears in pricing/search snippet context.

Interpretation:
- Not all clinics use nurse-first consultation.
- Doctor-led models may use nurse support after or around the doctor consult, but market themselves as higher-touch doctor care.

### Herbly

Herbly describes itself as involving doctors, health specialists and nurses. Its patient information page focuses on safe prescribing, doctor/nurse-practitioner explanation, careful assessment, interactions, contraindications, GP health summaries and follow-up.

Source:
- https://herbly.com.au/pages/patient-information-for-medicinal-cannabis-treatment

Relevant extracted points:
- patients should tell the doctor about prescription medicines, OTC products, supplements, alcohol, nicotine and other substance use
- contraindications include psychosis/bipolar, unstable cardiovascular disease, MI/angina, pregnancy/breastfeeding, under 18, significant liver/kidney disease and respiratory disease
- GP health summary may be requested where other prescription medicines are involved

Interpretation:
- In regulated product clinics, a pre-doctor support/triage layer is useful because many patients need education, document collection and safety screening before prescribing can be considered.

### Quit Clinics / QuitRX

Nicotine clinics reviewed generally use structured questionnaire + age/ID verification + doctor review/contact models. QuitRX explicitly lists questionnaire, doctor review, doctor call timeslot, and prescription if clinically appropriate. Quit Clinics lists medical/smoking history, age verification, doctor review and doctor call.

Sources:
- https://www.quitclinics.com/how-it-works/
- https://www.quitclinics.com/product/subscription/
- https://quitrx.com.au/

Interpretation:
- Nicotine clinics may not always advertise a nurse-first call, but they still use intake/verification before the doctor to reduce doctor time and support safer prescribing.

## 3. Official compliance context

### Medical Board / Ahpra telehealth expectations

The Medical Board/Ahpra position is that doctors must not prescribe from asynchronous questionnaire-only workflows. They need a real-time consultation and must evaluate information used in assessment, even if collected by a nurse or third party.

Source:
- https://www.medicalboard.gov.au/Codes-Guidelines-Policies/Telehealth-consultations-with-patients.aspx
- https://www.ahpra.gov.au/Resources/Medicinal-cannabis-prescribing.aspx

Relevant points:
- confirm patient identity to the best of ability
- no prescribing without real-time consultation
- online chat/questionnaire-only prescribing is not supported
- doctor accepts responsibility for evaluating information gathered by others

Implication:
- A nurse does not replace the doctor.
- A nurse can gather/clarify information, but the doctor must still review it and make the decision.

### Ahpra medicinal cannabis guidance

Ahpra warns about poor medicinal-cannabis prescribing practices, including very short consultations, online questionnaires that coach patients, failure to assess mental health/substance use history, not checking identity, under-18 prescribing, excessive quantities and conflict-of-interest models.

Source:
- https://www.ahpra.gov.au/Resources/Medicinal-cannabis-prescribing.aspx

Relevant extracted points:
- practitioners must assess patients thoroughly
- a full assessment includes social, mental-health and substance-use history
- this cannot be achieved in a short online consultation
- patient demand is not an indication to prescribe
- prescribers must check other medicines and not prescribe excessive quantities
- prescribers must document informed consent for unapproved products

Implication:
- Nurse prescreen helps collect and organise the depth of information needed, but does not reduce the doctor’s responsibility.
- It can protect the clinic from appearing like a low-quality “few-minute consult” model.

### NMBA nurse standards

Nurses can work within scope to assess, support, educate, document and escalate, but must remain accountable and practise within competence/scope.

Sources:
- https://www.nursingmidwiferyboard.gov.au/Codes-Guidelines-Statements/Professional-standards.aspx
- https://www.nursingmidwiferyboard.gov.au/Codes-Guidelines-Statements/Professional-standards/registered-nurse-standards-for-practice.aspx

Implication:
- Nurse prescreen is appropriate if framed as support, triage, clarification and documentation.
- It should not be framed as diagnosis, approval or prescribing.

## 4. Why clinics actually do nurse-first workflows

### 4.1 Cost control

Yes — cost is a major reason.

Doctors are more expensive and harder to schedule than nurses. If every patient goes straight to a doctor, the doctor spends paid clinical time on:

- incomplete forms
- identity/contact issues
- patients who are obviously unsuitable
- missing medication lists
- missing documents
- basic education
- product/process questions
- appointment no-shows or confused patients

A nurse can handle much of this at lower cost, leaving the doctor to do the legally/clinically necessary decision-making.

### 4.2 Doctor throughput

The doctor’s consult can be shorter and more focused if the nurse has already:

- confirmed identity/contact details
- clarified reason for care
- checked current medicines/allergies
- flagged red flags
- summarised prior treatments
- gathered supporting documents
- explained process limitations
- confirmed patient understands prescription is not guaranteed

This is likely why Alternaleaf can advertise a 20-minute nurse consult and a separate 10-minute doctor consult.

### 4.3 Better triage

Nurse prescreen can route patients into:

- proceed to doctor
- missing information
- priority doctor review
- in-person/GP review recommended
- urgent care pathway
- unsuitable for service

This reduces wasted doctor appointments and reduces risk.

### 4.4 Better compliance optics

Regulators have criticised questionnaire-only, high-volume, low-quality prescribing models. A nurse layer helps demonstrate the clinic is collecting proper clinical information and providing human review before doctor consult.

Important: this is not enough by itself. The doctor must still do a real consultation and make the decision.

### 4.5 Patient education and conversion

A nurse call is often a softer first touchpoint:

- explains the process
- reduces anxiety
- sets expectations
- answers basic questions
- tells the patient what documents are needed
- avoids surprise doctor fees if obviously unsuitable

This likely improves patient conversion and satisfaction.

### 4.6 Risk and document collection

In cannabis clinics especially, the nurse can collect:

- GP health summary
- prior treatment history
- medication list
- mental health history
- substance-use history
- contraindication details
- consent/education confirmations

For nicotine/pouch clinics, the equivalent is:

- smoking/vaping/nicotine history
- prior first-line therapy attempts
- oral-health flags
- medicine interaction flags
- importation understanding
- quantity/use pattern concerns

## 5. Relevance to PouchCare

For PouchCare, a nurse-first workflow is not mandatory. The options are:

### Option A — Doctor-only MVP

Flow:
1. signup
2. phone/email verification
3. prescreen quiz
4. automated risk flags
5. doctor reviews intake
6. doctor calls patient
7. doctor decides

Pros:
- simplest
- cheapest to implement operationally
- fewer staff roles
- less scheduling complexity
- easier compliance ownership

Cons:
- doctors spend time clarifying basics
- more expensive per low-quality lead
- less patient hand-holding
- lower throughput

### Option B — Conditional nurse prescreen

Nurse only gets involved when:

- intake is incomplete or inconsistent
- medication interaction flags exist
- mental health concern
- pregnancy/breastfeeding
- oral-health issue
- identity/contact mismatch
- high quantity/request concern
- patient asks for help
- doctor requests more information

Pros:
- good balance for MVP
- avoids nurse cost on every patient
- helps doctors only where useful
- supports quality and risk management

Cons:
- requires triage logic
- requires nurse workflow/dashboard

### Option C — Nurse-first clinic model

Every patient has nurse prescreen before doctor.

Pros:
- best doctor efficiency
- strong patient support
- good for scale
- better notes/documentation before doctor
- commercially similar to Alternaleaf-style model

Cons:
- more operational complexity
- nurse payroll/contracting
- additional scheduling step
- risk if patients think nurse call means approval

---

## 6. Recommendation

For PouchCare MVP, use **Option B: conditional nurse prescreen**.

Reason:

- A nurse is not required for compliance if the doctor does a proper real-time consult.
- But nurse prescreen is valuable for flagged cases, messy intakes and patient support.
- It keeps cost lower than nurse-first for every patient while preserving a safety valve.

Recommended positioning:

> Some patients may be contacted by our clinical support team before their doctor consultation to clarify information, confirm details, or collect anything the doctor may need. This does not guarantee a prescription. The doctor makes the final clinical decision after consultation.

Avoid saying:

- approved by nurse
- eligible for prescription
- nurse approval
- script ready

Suggested internal status:

- `doctor_review_required`
- `nurse_clarification_required`
- `nurse_prescreen_completed_doctor_review_required`
- `priority_doctor_review_required`
- `in_person_or_gp_review_recommended`

---

## 7. Bottom line

Your assumption is mostly right: nurse-first workflows are partly about **cost and GP efficiency**.

But the better framing is:

> Nurses are used to make the doctor’s time safer, cleaner and more efficient — not to replace the doctor’s clinical judgement.

For PouchCare, nurse prescreen should be optional/conditional at MVP unless the operating model needs high-volume doctor efficiency from day one.
